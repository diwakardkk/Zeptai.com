Healthcare AI should not be judged only by model performance. In clinical settings, trust depends on governance: who is accountable, how the system is monitored, and whether humans can understand and challenge outputs.

WHO's guidance on ethics and governance of artificial intelligence for health is one of the clearest frameworks available. It emphasizes protecting autonomy, promoting human well-being, ensuring transparency, fostering responsibility, and supporting sustainability.

## What this means for product teams

Responsible healthcare AI should answer simple operational questions:

- who reviews outputs?
- how are errors escalated?
- what is logged for audit?
- where does human override happen?
- how is patient privacy protected?

Those questions are as important as model architecture.

## Why this fits ZeptAI's published work

Your two papers strengthen this governance story in different ways. The mental health paper shows a structured, explainable hybrid workflow rather than a black-box conversational system. The chest X-ray paper focuses on interpretability directly, which supports transparency in imaging decisions.

That matters because responsible AI is easier to build when explainability is part of the design, not a patch added later.

## Trust is built in layers

In practice, healthcare AI trust depends on:

- evidence from peer-reviewed work
- reliable product behavior
- transparent outputs
- human-led oversight

A product that combines these layers is much more likely to be adopted than a system that only offers impressive benchmark numbers.
